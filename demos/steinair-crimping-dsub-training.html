D-sub connectors carry signals for navigation, communication, and flight instruments in virtually every avionics installation. The machined pins inside these connectors must be crimped correctly — a bad crimp can cause intermittent failures that are extremely dangerous in flight.

This module walks through the complete crimping process using real shop footage from SteinAir, covering tool selection, wire preparation, the witness hole check, and proper crimp verification.

1Strip the Wire

Strip the wire to the correct length for the pin you're using. Too short and you won't get engagement; too long and insulation won't seat properly.

Wire stripped to correct length, ready for pin insertion
2Insert Wire into Pin

Grab a pin and slide the stripped wire into the colored band end. Push it in until the wire is visible through the witness hole — a small hole on the side of the pin barrel.

The witness hole is your quality check. If you can't see copper through it, the wire isn't seated deep enough. This same check applies whether you're crimping or soldering.

Verify wire engagement through the witness hole before crimping
3Crimp the Pin

Open the crimper (import or certified — same result). Insert the pin until it hits the bottom of the positioner. Squeeze until the tool fully cycles and releases.

The crimp lands between the colored bands on the pin. You'll see four indent marks all the way around — that's your confirmation of a proper 4-sided crimp.

Pin inserted into crimper — squeeze until it cycles completely
4Verify & Repeat

Inspect the finished crimp. You should see four crimp marks evenly spaced around the barrel, positioned between the bands. If the crimps are misaligned or incomplete, your tool positioner may be wrong or the pin wasn't fully seated.

Repeat for each wire. Works the same for male and female pins.

Quick identification: Look at the connector shell.

• Two rows of pins = Standard density
• Three rows of pins = High density

The pins themselves are physically different sizes — don't mix them. Your crimper's positioner must match.

Gendering rule: The connector is gendered by the pins inside, not the shell shape. Pins sticking out = male. Sockets (holes) = female. Always.

Standard density (2 rows) vs. high density (3 rows) — pin size differs
  • Using a hex crimper — Hex crimps are for coax, not D-sub pins. You need the 4-way indent.
  • Skipping the witness hole check — If the wire isn't visible through the witness hole, it's not seated. The crimp will fail.
  • Wrong positioner installed — Standard and high-density pins need different positioners. The tool won't tell you it's wrong.
  • Not letting the tool fully cycle — Releasing early gives an incomplete crimp. Squeeze until it clicks open.
  • Crimping in the wrong spot — The crimp must land between the colored bands. Above or below compromises the connection.
